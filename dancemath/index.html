What is the Universal Unit System?

The Universal Unit System (UUS) is a method created by dance legend Skippy Blair that allows dancers to "read" and analyze dance rhythms much like a musician reads sheet music. While most dance classes teach students to count to 8 (e.g., "5, 6, 7, 8"), Skippy Blair recognized that this often confuses students because the actual footwork doesn't always line up neatly with those 8 beats. Instead, she developed a system based on what your feet are actually doing in relation to the music.

The "Unit" (The 2-Beat Rule)

The core philosophy of the system is that almost all dance music and movement can be broken down into 2-beat increments, called "Units. Instead of trying to memorize a long, complex 8-beat pattern, the UUS treats a dance pattern like a sentence made up of small, 2-beat words. If you can understand these tiny 2-beat "words," you can string them together to create any complex dance pattern.

"Sound" vs. "Silence"

Blair's system simplifies dance into two states of being. To a dancer, "rhythm" is simply the arrangement of weight changes (steps) over time. Sound (â€¢): You take a step (a weight change). Silence (/): You pause or hold (no weight change).

The Rolling Count ("& a 1, & a 2")

Most people count "1, 2, 3, 4." Blair argued this makes dancers "plod" or march because they only move exactly when the number is spoken. The Rolling Count inserts sounds between the beats to keep the body moving continuously:

    "& a 1, & a 2"
  • The "&": This is when your Center Point of Balance (CPB) moves. Your body starts moving before your foot steps.
  • The "a": This is when your foot releases from the floor.
  • The "1": This is when your foot strikes the floor (the beat).
    • The Rolling Count makes movement continuous like a wave rather than robotic like a ticking clock.
